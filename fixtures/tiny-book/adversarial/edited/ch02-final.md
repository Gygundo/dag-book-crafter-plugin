# Chapter 2: The Kitchen and the Desk

There was another Tuesday. The house was quiet again, quiet in that same quiet way, and I found myself pulled toward the same room. I stood at the kitchen counter with both hands flat on the wood and nothing spiritual to say. I had come again to the end of something and I had no words for it, and the silence that Tuesday felt less like absence and more like a room I had been invited into and forgotten how to enter.

Let me tell you what that felt like, in slightly different words, because I want you to see it from the other side. I planted both palms on the butcher block and I had nothing — nothing devotional, nothing theological, nothing holy — to offer up about it. The kitchen counter scene was not a metaphor. It was a Tuesday, and I was standing at the kitchen counter, and I had nothing spiritual to say.

Later I moved to the study. I sat down at my desk and turned on the desk lamp glowing yellow over the open notebook. The desk lamp glowing yellow was the only working light in the room, and I bent my head toward it the way plants bend toward whatever they can find.

I wrote one line. Then I wrote another. And then, without meaning to, I wrote the same sentence I had written in the foreword, the one I could not shake: one small lamp refusing the whole dark. I underlined it. I circled it. I sat with it under the desk lamp glowing yellow until the circling stopped feeling like a prayer and started feeling like an answer.

That is how the second chapter starts. Not with clarity. With a kitchen counter, and a desk lamp, and an underlined sentence on a page.
