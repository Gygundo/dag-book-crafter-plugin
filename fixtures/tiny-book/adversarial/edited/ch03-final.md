# Chapter 3: The Reading Lamp

By the third week I had moved the whole conversation to the bedroom. Specifically, to the reading lamp on the nightstand, a squat brass thing with a tilted shade and a bulb that hummed if you listened for it. The reading lamp on the nightstand became my new sanctuary, which is a strange word for a piece of furniture, but sanctuaries are made of whatever holds still while you fall apart next to them.

I would come home at the end of the day, drop my bag, sit on the edge of the bed, and click the reading lamp on the nightstand into its small soft yes. I would sit there for a long time without doing anything that anyone watching would call prayer.

The weight of it pressed on my chest the same way it had pressed in the first chapter, because griefs do not politely leave just because you have written about them once. They come back. They visit. They pull up the same chair.

But here is what I noticed in the third week that I had not noticed in the first: the weight of it pressed on my chest differently under the reading lamp than it had under the ceiling dark. Under the reading lamp, the weight was witnessed. And a witnessed weight is a divided weight, which is almost, in some arithmetic I am only beginning to understand, a halved one.

I turned off the reading lamp at some point that night. I do not remember when. I remember only that the room held me after the lamp went out, which is another way of saying I had finally let the room try.
